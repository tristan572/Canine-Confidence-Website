Introduction

The First Week Sets the Foundation

Bringing home a rescue dog is exciting. It is also, for the dog, one of the most disorienting experiences of their life. A new home, new smells, new people, new rules — all at once, with no context.

Most owners respond to this with love and freedom. They want the dog to know it is safe. They let the dog explore everywhere, sleep anywhere, and do whatever feels natural.

That instinct is understandable. It is also, in most cases, the thing that makes the first month much harder than it needs to be.

The biggest mistake new rescue owners make is giving too much freedom too soon. Structure is not a punishment. It is the thing that tells a dog: this place is safe, predictable, and mine.

This guide gives you the four principles that make the first week work. They are simple. They are free. And they make everything that comes after significantly easier.

Principle 1 of 4

Management First. Training Later.

Why it matters

Prevent problems before they start

Every problem you prevent is a habit that never forms. Management tools are not punishment — they are prevention. They stop problems before they start, which means less stress for the dog, less frustration for you, and no bad habits to undo.

  • Prevent, do not react. Every problem you prevent is a habit that never forms.
  • Freedom is earned, not given. A dog who earns access to the house respects it. A dog handed full access on Day 1 will test every boundary.
  • Structure creates freedom. The more structure now, the more genuine freedom later.
How to do it

The Drag Lead Protocol

  • Attach a lightweight 0.5–1 metre lead (handle cut off) to your dog's collar indoors
  • Do not hold it — just let it drag. It gives you easy, low-stress control without grabbing or chasing
  • Remove it when you cannot supervise (crate or pen instead)
  • Use it for the first 2–4 weeks while your dog learns the rules of the house
Also use

The Two-Room Rule

  • Restrict your dog to 1–2 rooms for the first week
  • Use baby gates or doors to block off the rest of the house
  • Expand access gradually as they demonstrate calm, settled behaviour
  • A dog who earns each room respects the whole house more
Principle 2 of 4

Build a Daily Rhythm

Dogs thrive on predictability. A simple daily rhythm removes guesswork for your dog and decision fatigue for you. It does not need to be rigid — just consistent enough that your dog starts to know what comes next.

Predictability is the fastest path to a calm dog. When a dog knows what is coming, they stop scanning for threats and start relaxing into the day.

Morning

  • Toilet break immediately on waking (before any food or attention)
  • Structured walk (20–30 min depending on breed/age)
  • Breakfast in the crate or designated spot
  • Rest period — do not engage immediately after eating

Midday

  • Short toilet break
  • Low-key enrichment (sniff work, Kong, chew)
  • Rest in crate or pen

Afternoon / Evening

  • Second walk or structured play session
  • Dinner in the crate or designated spot
  • Calm wind-down — avoid high-energy play in the hour before bed
  • Final toilet break, then settle for the night
Principle 3 of 4

Understand the Decompression Window

Most rescue dogs are not "themselves" for the first 2–12 weeks. They are in survival mode — reading every cue, suppressing natural behaviour, trying to work out if this place is permanent.

This is called the decompression window. It is normal. It is not a personality assessment.

  • ✓Shut down, low energy, not very interested in food or play — this is decompression, not sadness. Give them time.
  • ✓Overly hyper, bouncing off walls, can't settle — also decompression. Often mistaken for "high energy dog." Structure and rest fix this faster than more exercise.
  • ✓Perfect the first week, then "falls apart" in week two or three — this is the dog realising they are staying. Their real personality emerges. Totally normal.
  • ✓Showing fear, avoidance, or low-level aggression — do not push through it with forced socialisation. Give space, use management, let them set the pace.

Do not assess your dog's personality in the first month. You are seeing their coping strategy, not who they are.
